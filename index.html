pH Calculator

Formula
pH = -log[H⁺]
pH measures how acidic or basic a solution is, on a scale defined as the negative base-10 logarithm of the hydrogen ion concentration. Lower pH = more acidic, higher pH = more basic.
mol/L
(unitless)
🧫

Enter the hydrogen ion concentration, then press Calculate to find the pH.

Result
—
—
—
Step-by-Step Solution

pH is a logarithmic measure of the hydrogen ion concentration in a solution, defined as pH = -log₁₀[H⁺]. Because the scale is logarithmic, each whole-number change in pH represents a tenfold change in [H⁺].

At 25 °C, pure water has [H⁺] = 1 × 10⁻⁷ M, giving pH = 7 (neutral). Solutions with pH < 7 are acidic (higher [H⁺]); solutions with pH > 7 are basic / alkaline (lower [H⁺]).

pH
The acidity/basicity of a solution. Dimensionless — has no unit.
[H⁺]
Hydrogen ion (hydronium, H₃O⁺) concentration, measured in mol/L (M).
  • Forgetting the negative sign in pH = -log[H⁺], leading to the wrong sign in the result.
  • Using natural log (ln) instead of base-10 log (log).
  • Treating pH as a linear scale — a drop of 1 pH unit means a 10× increase in [H⁺], not 10%.
  • Entering [H⁺] in the wrong units (it must be mol/L, not mg/L or %).
  • Rounding [H⁺] too early, which introduces significant error after the log is taken.
  • Medicine: blood pH is tightly regulated to ~7.35–7.45; deviations indicate acidosis or alkalosis.
  • Agriculture: soil pH controls nutrient availability and crop health.
  • Water treatment: pH is adjusted in drinking water and swimming pools for safety and effectiveness.
  • Food & beverage: pH affects taste, preservation, and fermentation (e.g., wine, dairy).
  • Industry: wastewater neutralization and chemical manufacturing rely on precise pH control.
  • Memorize both forms: pH = -log[H⁺] and [H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ.
  • Remember pH + pOH = 14 at 25 °C — a fast way to cross-check answers.
  • Practice using the log and 10ˣ (antilog) functions on your calculator.
  • Always state whether the solution is acidic, neutral, or basic based on the pH value.
  • Show units clearly: pH has no unit, but [H⁺] is always in mol/L.

Step-by-Step Solution

pOH is a logarithmic measure of the hydroxide ion concentration in a solution, defined as pOH = -log₁₀[OH⁻]. Like pH, each whole-number change in pOH represents a tenfold change in [OH⁻].

At 25 °C, pure water has [OH⁻] = 1 × 10⁻⁷ M, giving pOH = 7 (neutral). Solutions with pOH < 7 are basic / alkaline (higher [OH⁻]); solutions with pOH > 7 are acidic (lower [OH⁻]) — the opposite trend to pH.

pOH
The basicity of a solution, on the same logarithmic scale as pH. Dimensionless — has no unit.
[OH⁻]
Hydroxide ion concentration, measured in mol/L (M).
  • Forgetting the negative sign in pOH = -log[OH⁻].
  • Confusing the direction of the scale — a lower pOH means a more basic solution, the opposite of pH.
  • Not using pH + pOH = 14 (at 25 °C) to cross-check a result.
  • Using natural log (ln) instead of base-10 log (log).
  • Entering [OH⁻] in the wrong units — it must be mol/L.
  • Cleaning products: ammonia and bleach-based cleaners rely on high [OH⁻] (low pOH) for effectiveness.
  • Antacids: formulated to raise stomach pH by supplying hydroxide or related basic ions.
  • Soap & detergent manufacturing: saponification requires strongly basic (low pOH) conditions.
  • Wastewater treatment: excess acidity is neutralized by adding bases, monitored via pOH/pH.
  • Aquaculture: maintaining safe [OH⁻]/pOH levels protects aquatic life.
  • Memorize both forms: pOH = -log[OH⁻] and [OH⁻] = 10⁻ᵖᴼᴴ.
  • Use pH + pOH = 14 at 25 °C to quickly find one from the other.
  • Remember the scale is inverted relative to pH: low pOH = basic, high pOH = acidic.
  • Double-check the sign of the exponent when converting [OH⁻] back from pOH.
  • State the result's units: pOH is unitless, [OH⁻] is in mol/L.

Hydrogen Ion Concentration Calculator

Formula
[H⁺] = Kw ÷ [OH⁻]
Ion Product of Water: [H⁺] × [OH⁻] = Kw = 1 × 10⁻¹⁴ (at 25 °C). Knowing one ion's concentration lets you find the other.
mol/L
mol/L
⚛️

Enter the known ion concentration, then press Calculate to find the other.

Result
—
—
—
Step-by-Step Solution

Water self-ionizes slightly: 2 H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH⁻. At equilibrium, the product of the hydrogen and hydroxide ion concentrations is a constant called the ion product of water, Kw.

At 25 °C, Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1 × 10⁻¹⁴. This relationship holds for all aqueous solutions, not just pure water — so if you know one ion's concentration, you can always find the other.

[H⁺]
Hydrogen ion (hydronium) concentration, in mol/L (M).
[OH⁻]
Hydroxide ion concentration, in mol/L (M).
Kw
Ion product of water — a constant equal to 1 × 10⁻¹⁴ at 25 °C (units M², usually omitted).
  • Assuming Kw = 1 × 10⁻¹⁴ at any temperature — it only holds at 25 °C; Kw increases with temperature.
  • Dividing in the wrong direction (e.g., computing [OH⁻] = [H⁺] ÷ Kw instead of Kw ÷ [H⁺]).
  • Mixing up which concentration was given vs. which is being solved for.
  • Forgetting that both concentrations must be positive — a negative or zero input is not physically valid.
  • Losing track of powers of ten when dividing very small numbers.
  • Buffer design: pharmaceutical and biological buffers rely on the H⁺/OH⁻ balance to maintain stable pH.
  • Titration analysis: finding [H⁺] or [OH⁻] at any point helps determine the equivalence point.
  • Environmental science: ocean acidification studies track changes in [H⁺] as CO₂ dissolves in seawater.
  • Biochemistry: enzyme activity is highly sensitive to ion concentrations near the active site.
  • Cross-checking: verifying pH/pOH calculations by confirming [H⁺][OH⁻] = Kw.
  • Memorize [H⁺][OH⁻] = Kw = 1 × 10⁻¹⁴ (at 25 °C).
  • To find the missing ion, simply divide: unknown = Kw ÷ known.
  • Use this relationship to verify answers from pH/pOH calculations — they should be mutually consistent.
  • Keep track of significant figures and powers of ten carefully.
  • Remember: as one ion concentration increases, the other must decrease proportionally.

pH + pOH Relation Calculator

Formula
pH + pOH = 14
At 25 °C, the sum of pH and pOH always equals 14 (derived from Kw = 1 × 10⁻¹⁴). Enter either value to find the other.
(unitless)
(unitless)
⚖️

Enter either pH or pOH, then press Calculate to find the other.

Result
—
—
—
Step-by-Step Solution

At 25 °C, the ion product of water is Kw = 1 × 10⁻¹⁴. Taking the negative log of both sides gives -log(Kw) = -log[H⁺] + (-log[OH⁻]), which simplifies to pKw = pH + pOH = 14.

This means that in any aqueous solution at 25 °C, pH and pOH are complementary — knowing one instantly gives the other by simple subtraction from 14.

pH
Negative log of [H⁺]. Dimensionless. Acidic < 7, Neutral = 7, Basic > 7.
pOH
Negative log of [OH⁻]. Dimensionless. Basic < 7, Neutral = 7, Acidic > 7.
14
pKw at 25 °C — the negative log of 1 × 10⁻¹⁴.
  • Using pH + pOH = 14 at temperatures other than 25 °C — pKw changes with temperature.
  • Adding pH and pOH and expecting 7 (neutral), rather than 14.
  • Confusing the relation with pH × pOH = 14 — it's a sum, not a product.
  • Entering a pH or pOH outside 0–14 without checking whether the value is physically reasonable.
  • Quick cross-check: if pH is measured directly, pOH = 14 − pH gives [OH⁻] without additional measurement.
  • Titration curves: equivalence points and buffer regions are analyzed using both pH and pOH.
  • Blood chemistry: normal blood pH 7.35–7.45 implies pOH 6.55–6.65 — useful in clinical calculations.
  • Education: this relation is a cornerstone of acid-base equilibrium problems in JEE, NEET, and CBSE exams.
  • Always state the temperature (25 °C) when writing pH + pOH = 14.
  • Use this relation to quickly verify other acid-base calculations.
  • Remember: both pH and pOH are unitless quantities.
  • At neutrality (25 °C): pH = pOH = 7. Any deviation from 7 in one shifts the other by the same amount in the opposite direction.
